Pics

All images are edited in HDR and exported to JPEG with HDR gain map. All images are also signed with C2PA (Content Credentials).

All photos on this site follow strict photojournalistic rules for editing:

  • Allowed: Exposure and other contrast adjustments. White balance and tint adjustments. Color to B&W conversion.
  • Minor adjustments allowed: Sharpening, saturation, noise reduction, local adjustments of color and contrast.
  • Disallowed: AI upscaling, AI noise reduction, spot healing, dust removal, geometric corrections, panoramic stitching, HDR image merging, artificial blurring.
  • Not recommended: Lens correction: often makes noise look worse.

In general, the rules allow for changes that happen on an individual pixel level, but not changes that alter the pixel structure. The only exceptions being mild noise reduction to improve web compression, and manufacturer-standardized lens corrections because it can be considered an alternative native rendering of the image.

Local adjustments of color should never be adjusted to a degree where the color of a person or an object becomes misleading in terms of what that object looks like in real life. That said, cameras never capture the world exactly like our eyes and the viewer should assume that no pictorial representation of reality will 100% match the experience of seeing something in real life.

These images are published with C2PA signing to prove that the images are real, or at least as close to real as an image can be. To check this, you can download the image and check it using this Content Credentials page.

Images are also published in HDR, using HDR gain maps in JPEG to be backwards compatible with non-HDR browsers and displays. Publishing images in HDR is to more closely represent the dynamic range of the real world where the picture was captured. What you see may still be affected by the projection quality of your viewing device. So far, no camera can capture and no display can project the true dynamic range of the real world. And likely never will. Still, an HDR rendering of an image is a lot closer to representing perceptual reality than an SDR image.
